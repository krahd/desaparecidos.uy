# desaparecidos.uy

**A series of computational memorial artworks**  
**Draft project document**  
**Tomas Laurenzo**  
**17 June 2026**

---

## 1. Project overview

`desaparecidos.uy` is a series of computational artworks in which the image of each disappeared person is reassembled from fragments of the country that survived them: its people, its places, and its visual infrastructure.

A main guiding artistic goal is to computationally reference the continuing search for the disappeared.

The project proposes a triptych of related works:

1. **Todos somos familiares** — disappeared persons’ public images are reassembled from fragments extracted from publicly available images of people in Uruguay.
2. **Están en todas partes** — disappeared persons’ public images are reassembled from fragments extracted from images of Uruguay’s places, surfaces, streets, walls, landscapes, and material environments.
3. **Seguimos buscando** — disappeared persons’ public images are reassembled through a process of traversal, search, and visual scanning across Uruguay’s contemporary image field.

These works are not photo mosaics of the disappeared. They are computational memorial systems in which the present visual field of Uruguay—its people, places, streets, and publicly circulating images—is decomposed into fragments and made to participate in the reappearance of those whom state terror attempted to erase.

The works reconstruct the public images of Uruguay’s disappeared from fragments of the country that remains: its faces, its streets, its walls, its landscapes, its images. The reconstruction is deliberately incomplete. It does not claim to restore what was destroyed, nor to replace archival or judicial truth. Instead, it makes visible a political condition: disappearance is not an absence located in the past, but a structure distributed through the present. The faces emerge from the living social body and from the material surface of the nation, insisting that the disappeared are not outside us, but among us, within us, and everywhere.

The project is conceived simultaneously as a web artwork, a set of generated videos, a possible installation, and a research-based artistic system. It is not an archive, a forensic tool, a historical database, or an institutional memorial in the conventional sense. It is an artistic apparatus for making perceptible the persistence of disappearance within the present.

## 2. Historical and political horizon

The project addresses the detenidos desaparecidos connected to Uruguay’s civic-military dictatorship and to the broader regional apparatus of repression that operated across the Southern Cone. It begins from the understanding that enforced disappearance is not only the removal of a person from social life, but also an attack on evidence, kinship, narration, mourning, and historical continuity.

The disappeared are not simply missing. They were actively made unavailable: to their families, to legal processes, to public knowledge, to their own biographies, and to the ordinary forms through which societies recognize the living and mourn the dead. Their disappearance continues as long as their fate remains unresolved, as long as institutional archives remain incomplete, as long as perpetrators withhold information, and as long as public life treats absence as a settled historical fact rather than as an ongoing political structure.

`desaparecidos.uy` does not attempt to solve that absence. It does not pretend to know what remains unknown. It does not reconstruct bodies, deaths, places of burial, chains of command, or historical facts beyond the publicly documented record. Instead, it asks how computational art can construct a visual condition in which absence is made active: a condition in which each image of a disappeared person appears through the living and material country that continues after them.

The project therefore treats Uruguay itself as the image field of the work. The country is not represented as background, landscape, or national symbol. It becomes the material support through which the disappeared reappear. The work’s central claim is not that the disappeared can be restored, but that the present cannot be understood without them.

## 3. The triptych

### 3.1. Todos somos familiares

**Todos somos familiares** reconstructs the public images of disappeared persons from fragments extracted from publicly available images of contemporary Uruguayans.

The title is deliberately direct. It does not mean that all Uruguayans have the same relationship to disappearance, nor does it flatten the specific historical, familial, and political labour of relatives’ organizations. Rather, it states an ethical and political demand: the disappeared cannot remain the responsibility of their biological families alone. Disappearance reorganizes the entire social body. It creates a debt that is collective, even when the pain is not equally distributed.

In this work, contemporary faces become visual material from which the disappeared are partially reassembled. The system does not identify the living persons whose images contribute fragments. It does not classify them, name them, infer attributes from them, or represent them as individuals. Their images enter the work only as dispersed visual matter: colour, tone, texture, shadow, contour, surface, and partial form.

The work should therefore be understood less as a portrait mosaic than as a political condensation of visual responsibility. The disappeared person’s face emerges from the living social field. The face is not rebuilt from a family album, nor only from state or human-rights archives, but from the public visual surface of the country that inherited the unresolved consequences of disappearance.

The work stages a difficult relation between intimacy and collectivity. Faces are among the most personal of images, yet they are also among the most socially circulated. The piece uses this tension without converting it into identification. It asks: if the disappeared are not only the concern of their families, what does it mean for the living social body to become the material through which they reappear?

The final form may be a generated video sequence, an interactive web artwork, or an installation. In the video version, each disappeared person gradually appears from many small fragments. The reconstruction remains visibly unstable. The fragments never disappear fully into seamless representation. The viewer should perceive both the face and the impossibility of restoring it.

### 3.2. Están en todas partes

**Están en todas partes** reconstructs the public images of disappeared persons from fragments extracted from images of Uruguay’s places: walls, streets, buildings, pavements, rural roads, institutional facades, river edges, landscapes, ruins, monuments, neighbourhoods, and ordinary surfaces.

If **Todos somos familiares** begins from the social body, **Están en todas partes** begins from the material body of the country. It treats the disappeared as distributed through the surfaces of national space. The work does not claim that every place is directly connected to every disappearance. Its claim is more structural: disappearance reshapes the political meaning of place. It marks the country as a whole, not only the specific sites where detention, torture, burial, testimony, or commemoration occurred.

The work’s source images may include streets, walls, public buildings, paths, industrial structures, domestic exteriors, vegetation, monuments, beaches, sidewalks, windows, doors, signs, and other visual surfaces. These images are decomposed into fragments and recomposed into the face of a disappeared person. The face appears, but it appears as something made from the country’s surfaces.

The conceptual operation is important. The work does not place a portrait over a landscape. It does not superimpose memory onto place. Instead, place becomes the material out of which memory temporarily takes form. The disappeared appear from the country rather than against it.

This work is likely the most robust first prototype. It has lower privacy risk than the face-based work, while preserving the core conceptual structure of the project. It also allows the visual system to develop a distinctive language: texture, erosion, architecture, geography, urban decay, rural dispersion, institutional austerity, and everyday surfaces can all become computational material for memorial reassembly.

The final images should not become too polished. The face should remain dependent on the fragments that compose it. The work should preserve the difference between portrait and ground, between person and country, between reconstruction and loss. Its power depends on a careful balance: the disappeared person must become visible, but the country from which the image is made must also remain present.

### 3.3. Seguimos buscando

**Seguimos buscando** transforms the act of search into the temporal structure of the work.

Where the first two pieces focus on reconstruction from particular classes of visual material—people and places—this third work foregrounds traversal. It imagines the country as an image field to be scanned, crossed, searched, and revisited. The work may use street-level imagery, self-captured footage, archival routes, crowdsourced visual material, or other forms of lawful and ethically reviewed visual traversal. Its central gesture is not the static composition of a portrait, but the process by which a face emerges from movement through the country.

While in the three pieces the searching itself is  an important compontent of the work, it is in **Seguimos buscando** where it is surfaced more explicitly. 

The title avoids naming the search as complete. It also avoids assigning the search only to the state, only to families, or only to institutions. “Seguimos” is deliberately plural. It implies persistence, but also insufficiency. The search continues because the truth remains incomplete.

The work may take the form of a video in which a virtual or actual camera moves through roads, cities, towns, rural spaces, and public visual material. During this movement, fragments are collected, compared, selected, and assembled. A face begins to form. At some point the image becomes recognizable enough to identify the disappeared person by name, date, and minimal public biographical information. The face then dissolves, and the search resumes.

This structure matters because it resists closure. Each reconstruction is temporary. Each completed portrait immediately returns the viewer to incompletion. The system keeps searching, not because the algorithm needs more data, but because the political condition remains unresolved.

**Seguimos buscando** should therefore operate as the temporal and processual anchor of the triptych. It connects the visual method to the historical labour of searching: searching archives, searching military records, searching landscapes, searching testimony, searching remains, searching for truth, searching for responsibility.

## 4. What the works are not

The project requires precise negative definitions. These exclusions are not defensive. They are part of the work’s method.

The works are **not forensic reconstructions**. They do not claim to improve the historical image of a disappeared person, to recover unknown facial details, or to produce an evidentiary image.

The works are **not documentary archives**. They may refer to archival sources and public records, but they do not replace them. The authority of the historical record remains with families, human-rights organizations, historians, judicial processes, official investigative bodies, and existing archives.

The works are **not biometric systems**. They do not identify living persons. They do not classify faces. They do not infer demographic or behavioural categories. They do not create a searchable database of people.

The works are **not deepfakes or resurrection media**. They do not animate the disappeared as if they were alive. They do not synthesize speech, fabricate testimony, or simulate subjectivity.

The works are **not monuments to technological power**. The system should not present computation as the force that restores what politics destroyed. The work must remain clear that technology cannot repair disappearance. At most, it can produce a visual and political structure through which the unresolved condition of disappearance becomes newly perceptible.

Finally, the works are **not merely photo mosaics**. They may use mosaic-like visual operations, and they belong to a lineage that includes portrait mosaics and memorial photographic assemblage. However, their primary operation is not the production of a large image from many smaller images. Their primary operation is the decomposition of the present visual field of a country and its recomposition into a memorial encounter with the disappeared.

## 5. Visual and computational method

The system begins with public images of disappeared persons. These target images should be drawn from responsible public sources and accompanied by provenance metadata. The system should record, at minimum, the source, date of access, image status, person’s name, and any public biographical details included in the source.

The source fields for reconstruction differ across the three works:

- **Todos somos familiares** uses fragments from publicly available images of contemporary people in Uruguay.
- **Están en todas partes** uses fragments from images of places, surfaces, landscapes, buildings, streets, and material environments.
- **Seguimos buscando** uses traversal-based imagery: routes, sequences, street-level views, self-captured footage, or other moving visual fields.

The computational process can be described in six stages.

### 5.1. Target preparation

Each disappeared person’s public image is prepared as a target. The goal is not to enhance, beautify, or complete the image. The system may normalize scale, contrast, and alignment only to the extent required for the reconstruction process. The historical image should remain the referential anchor.

Where possible, each target should be accompanied by a minimal textual layer: name, date of birth if public, date and place of disappearance if public, and source. This text should be restrained. The image should not become an illustrated database entry. The work’s central encounter remains visual, but the person’s name must appear.

### 5.2. Source acquisition

The source image field is collected according to the rules of each work. In the prototype phase, acquisition may be broad, but it should remain documented. For any public release, source acquisition must be reviewed legally and ethically.

For **Todos somos familiares**, the system may crawl publicly available images, but the crawler should not be designed to identify people. The purpose of acquisition is visual fragmentation, not personhood extraction. Public availability should not be treated as equivalent to unrestricted consent. The project’s release version should be shaped by legal review, especially because face imagery may fall within personal-data and biometric-data regimes depending on processing.

For **Están en todas partes**, source acquisition should privilege self-captured, licensed, institutional, public-domain, or clearly reusable imagery of places. This work can be developed first because its privacy risks are significantly lower.

For **Seguimos buscando**, source acquisition should favour self-captured footage, commissioned footage, institutional permissions, or open street-level imagery whose licensing and reuse terms have been reviewed. If proprietary street-view material is used during early experiments, it should remain internal until legal review determines whether and how it may be released.

### 5.3. Fragment extraction

The source image field is decomposed into fragments. These fragments may be rectangular patches, irregular patches, texture samples, colour fields, edge fields, or other visual units.

In **Todos somos familiares**, fragment extraction should be designed to reduce identifiability. The system may store face-derived fragments, but the project’s internal rule is that it does not identify, classify, or represent the living persons whose publicly available images may have contributed visual fragments. It treats the web as a dispersed visual field, not as a biometric archive.

Possible privacy-preserving design choices include:

- avoiding storage of full source faces in the release pipeline;
- storing fragments rather than complete portraits wherever feasible;
- limiting the size of contiguous fragments;
- avoiding large recognizable crops of eyes, mouths, or full facial structures;
- preventing any single source image from contributing too much to any final portrait;
- keeping source identity separated from visual fragments;
- documenting and enforcing deletion policies for raw source material;
- excluding minors, private contexts, sensitive contexts, and images connected to health, criminal justice, education, or political participation unless explicit permission exists.

These constraints are not merely compliance measures. They support the work’s conceptual distinction between a dispersed visual field and a database of persons.

### 5.4. Matching and assembly

The reconstruction process compares regions of the target portrait with fragments from the source field. Matching may use colour, luminance, gradient, edge direction, texture, local contrast, spatial composition, perceptual similarity, learned embeddings, or combinations of these methods. The system should not require semantic identification of source persons or places.

The assembly should remain visible as assembly. The final image should not fully conceal the fragments. The viewer should be able to perceive that the face is made from elsewhere: from other faces, from walls, from streets, from surfaces, from the country.

The system should allow multiple reconstructions of the same person. This variability is conceptually important. It prevents the work from producing a definitive substitute image. Each output is one possible temporary apparition, not a replacement for the historical photograph.

### 5.5. Temporal rendering

The project’s most important final form may be video. A still image can show the conceptual operation, but video can show the process of reappearance.

A typical sequence might proceed as follows:

1. fragments appear as unorganized visual matter;
2. the system begins to sort, move, compare, and place them;
3. a face slowly becomes perceptible;
4. the portrait reaches a fragile moment of recognition;
5. the person’s name and minimal public data appear;
6. the image destabilizes, dissolves, or returns to the visual field;
7. the next reconstruction begins.

This temporal structure prevents the face from becoming a resolved object. The work should not end in restoration, but in continued search.

### 5.6. Output forms

The project can exist in several related forms:

- a web artwork at `desaparecidos.uy`;
- a series of generated videos;
- a multi-channel installation;
- large-format prints or lightboxes showing selected reconstructions;
- an interactive kiosk or projection work;
- a documented open-source software system with restricted or synthetic sample data;
- a research paper or artist statement describing the method, lineage, and ethical framework.

The web version should not simply display finished images. It should expose the system’s logic: source categories, method, limits, privacy principles, relation to archives, and acknowledgement of visual/artistic precedents.

## 6. Website structure

The website should be minimal, sober, and explicit. It should avoid spectacle. Its visual language should be closer to a memorial apparatus, research interface, and generative artwork than to a portfolio or data visualization dashboard.

A possible structure:

- `/` — project introduction and triptych overview;
- `/todos-somos-familiares` — face-fragment work;
- `/estan-en-todas-partes` — place-fragment work;
- `/seguimos-buscando` — traversal/search work;
- `/personas` — public list of disappeared persons included, with source links;
- `/metodo` — computational and artistic method;
- `/etica` — privacy, data, legal, and release commitments;
- `/fuentes` — historical, archival, and visual-source provenance;
- `/linaje` — relation to prior artworks, especially Juan Ángel Urruzola’s *Álbum de memoria*;
- `/creditos` — collaborators, institutions, acknowledgements;
- `/contacto` — contact and opt-out/removal requests.

The `/etica` page should be written before public release, not after. It should state clearly what the system does and does not do. It should also include a plain-language explanation of how living persons’ images are treated, what is stored, what is discarded, and how concerns may be raised.

## 7. Artistic lineage and originality

The project should acknowledge Juan Ángel Urruzola’s *Álbum de memoria* as a relevant Uruguayan precedent. In that work, small identification-style photographs of detained-disappeared persons were assembled into the enlarged face of another disappeared person. Tatiana Oroño described the work as a “mosaic of tiny faces” in which the enlarged image of one disappeared person appears through many others.[^urruzola]

This precedent is important, and it should be treated as lineage rather than obstacle. `desaparecidos.uy` differs from *Álbum de memoria* in its source field, computational method, and political operation. Urruzola’s work forms the face of one disappeared person from images of other disappeared persons. `desaparecidos.uy` forms the public images of the disappeared from fragments of contemporary Uruguay: living faces, material surfaces, streets, walls, landscapes, and public visual infrastructures.

The difference is not only technical. It is conceptual. In Urruzola’s work, the disappeared visually sustain one another. In `desaparecidos.uy`, the disappeared emerge from the country that remains. The work therefore shifts from memorial co-presence among the disappeared to an implicating relation between disappearance and the present social and material body of Uruguay.

The project also belongs to a broader history of photographic mosaics, computational assemblage, and new media appropriation. Its originality does not lie in the mere fact of composing a large image from smaller images. Its originality lies in the combination of:

- Uruguayan enforced disappearance as historical and political focus;
- the use of the country’s contemporary visual field as source material;
- a triptych structure moving from people, to places, to traversal;
- a computational system that stages reappearance as process rather than product;
- a privacy framework that rejects identification, classification, and biometric archiving;
- an insistence on incompletion rather than restoration.

The project should therefore avoid claiming formal novelty in the abstract. It should claim a specific artistic and political operation: a computational memorial system that makes the disappeared appear from the unresolved present.

## 8. Ethical and privacy framework

The privacy framework must be part of the project from the beginning, even if legal review occurs before public release.

The working principle for **Todos somos familiares** is:

> The work may store face-derived visual fragments, but it does not identify, classify, or represent the living persons whose publicly available images may have contributed visual fragments. It treats the web as a dispersed visual field, not as a biometric archive.

This principle should guide the prototype. Before public release, it should be reviewed with university counsel or appropriate legal specialists, especially under Uruguayan personal-data law and any applicable institutional review processes.

During development, images that would not be part of the finished artworks may be stored.

The project should distinguish between three levels of material:

1. **Historical target images** — public images of disappeared persons, used as the referential portraits of the work.
2. **Source visual field** — images of people, places, or routes from which fragments are extracted.
3. **Generated outputs** — reconstructed portraits, videos, installations, and web presentations.

Each level requires different treatment.

Historical target images require careful provenance and respect. The project should avoid altering them in ways that imply correction, enhancement, animation, or resurrection.

Source images of living persons require privacy protection. Public availability should not be treated as blanket permission for any form of processing. Even when images are public, the project should minimize identifiability, avoid classification, avoid source-person representation, and make deletion or exclusion procedures available.

Generated outputs require careful review before publication. They should be inspected to ensure that no living person appears recognizably, no source image is reconstructable, and no output creates a false implication about the source persons.

The project should also avoid using images from contexts that create additional vulnerability or sensitivity. Unless explicit permission exists, the system should exclude images from schools, hospitals, prisons, shelters, protests, funerals, religious ceremonies, political meetings, or other contexts where visual presence may reveal sensitive personal information.

A public ethics statement should include:

- the project’s purpose;
- the distinction between visual fragments and person identification;
- what is collected;
- what is stored;
- what is discarded;
- what is never done;
- how opt-out or removal requests work;
- how legal and institutional review were conducted;
- how the work relates to existing archives and relatives’ organizations.

The release process should include legal review before publication. It may also require a data-protection impact assessment if the project’s processing is considered biometric under Uruguayan rules. Uruguay’s data-protection authority defines personal data broadly as information referring to determined or determinable persons, and Uruguayan Resolution 30/020 states that biometric-data processing requires a prior data-protection impact assessment.[^personaldata][^biometrics]

## 9. Relation to archives and public records

The project should not create a parallel authority over the disappeared. It should rely on existing public sources and cite them clearly.

Potential historical sources include:

- the Secretaría de Derechos Humanos para el Pasado Reciente, which provides personal files and lists of victims of forced disappearance;[^sdh]
- Madres y Familiares de Uruguayos Detenidos Desaparecidos, which maintains public materials, lists, biographies, posters, and memory resources;[^familiares]
- Sitios de Memoria Uruguay and related catalogues of sites, victims, and repressive infrastructures;[^sitios]
- public reports and historical investigations from relevant state, university, and human-rights institutions.

These sources should not simply be scraped and absorbed. They should be credited. Where possible, the project should link back to them. If collaboration or consultation becomes possible, it should be pursued, but the artistic project should remain clear about its own scope: it is an artwork, not an official archive.

The project’s person pages, if included, should remain restrained. They should not compete with existing biographical resources. Their purpose is to identify the person represented in the artwork and direct viewers toward authoritative sources.

## 10. Visual language

The visual language should avoid both excessive smoothness and excessive spectacle.

The reconstructions should remain incomplete, unstable, and materially legible. The viewer should perceive the tension between the face and the fragments. The image should be recognizable enough to name the disappeared person, but not so seamless that the work appears to have repaired the image.

This is especially important because the project deals with disappearance. A seamless reconstruction risks suggesting restoration, technological mastery, or closure. The correct visual condition is partial reappearance: a face made visible through fragments, yet still marked by loss.

The works should also avoid the sentimental codes of memorial imagery when possible. They should be sober, precise, and formally rigorous. The affective force should emerge from the system’s operation rather than from added melodrama.

Possible visual principles:

- keep fragments visible;
- avoid full photorealistic smoothing;
- allow gaps, seams, and mismatches;
- use time as a structure of emergence and dissolution;
- present names plainly;
- avoid heroic or sanctifying visual language;
- avoid animation that simulates life;
- avoid synthetic speech or invented testimony;
- preserve archival humility.

---

## 11. Sound

Sound should be treated carefully. The project does not require music in order to function, but sound may strengthen the installation or video versions if it avoids sentimental excess.

Possible approaches include:

- sparse low-frequency textures;
- quiet mechanical sounds derived from scanning, sorting, or traversal;
- processed environmental recordings from Uruguay;
- restrained use of silence;
- percussive or granular structures corresponding to fragment assembly;
- non-melodramatic tonal fields that support attention rather than dictate emotion.

The sound should not imitate mourning. It should not tell the viewer what to feel. It should support the temporal experience of searching, assembling, recognizing, and losing the image again.

## 12. Development stages

### Stage 1 — Place-based prototype

The first prototype should be **Están en todas partes**. It is conceptually strong, visually promising, and legally less complex than the face-based work. It can establish the core pipeline: target preparation, source ingestion, fragment extraction, matching, assembly, rendering, metadata, and output review.

Deliverables:

- a small set of target portraits;
- a curated image set of Uruguayan places and surfaces;
- a working reconstruction algorithm;
- several still outputs;
- one short video sequence;
- draft method and ethics pages.

### Stage 2 — Face-fragment prototype

The second prototype should be **Todos somos familiares**, developed internally. This stage should test the visual and conceptual power of reconstructing disappeared faces from fragments of contemporary public images of people.

Deliverables:

- crawler or controlled source-ingestion system;
- face-fragment extraction process;
- storage and deletion policy;
- identifiability risk tests;
- internal-only reconstructions;
- legal questions document for university counsel;
- revised privacy framework.

This stage should not be publicly released until legal review is complete.

### Stage 3 — Traversal prototype

The third prototype should be **Seguimos buscando**. It can build on the previous two systems but add sequence, movement, route, and search.

Deliverables:

- a visual traversal corpus;
- route-based or sequence-based reconstruction logic;
- one complete video loop;
- website prototype section;
- installation sketch.

### Stage 4 — Public release preparation

Before publication, the project should undergo a release review covering:

- image provenance;
- legal status of source material;
- privacy and biometric-data implications;
- removal/opt-out procedures;
- historical-source attribution;
- review by relevant institutional counsel;
- possible consultation with human-rights or memory organizations;
- final ethics statement;
- final credits and acknowledgements.

### Stage 5 — Exhibition and publication

The project can then be developed for:

- web launch;
- single-channel video;
- three-channel installation;
- public presentation in Uruguay;
- conference submission;
- journal article on computational memorial systems;
- open-source release of selected code, excluding sensitive data or raw source imagery.

## 13. Core statement

`desaparecidos.uy` is a computational memorial artwork series about the persistence of disappearance in Uruguay’s present. It reconstructs the public images of disappeared persons from fragments of the country that survived them: people, places, streets, walls, landscapes, and the visual infrastructures through which the nation appears to itself.

The project does not claim to recover the disappeared. It does not claim to restore archival truth, replace judicial investigation, or repair historical violence through technology. Its reconstructions are deliberately incomplete. They function as temporary apparitions: images that become visible only through the unresolved social and material field left behind.

The triptych moves across three forms of implication. **Todos somos familiares** makes the living social body participate in the image. **Están en todas partes** makes the country’s surfaces participate in the image. **Seguimos buscando** makes search itself become the work’s temporal form.

Together, the works insist that disappearance is not an absence sealed in the past. It remains distributed across bodies, places, institutions, images, and silences. The disappeared are not outside the present. They remain among us, within us, and everywhere.
